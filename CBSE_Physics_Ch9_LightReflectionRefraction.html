01 · REFLECTION OF LIGHT

Laws of Reflection & Plane Mirrors

🪞

Reflection of Light

A highly polished surface, such as a mirror, reflects most of the light falling on it. Light seems to travel in straight lines. A small source casts a sharp shadow of an opaque object — pointing to the straight-line path of light, usually indicated as a ray of light.

📐

Laws of Reflection

(i) The angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection.

(ii) The incident ray, the normal to the mirror at the point of incidence and the reflected ray, all lie in the same plane.

These laws are applicable to all types of reflecting surfaces including spherical surfaces.

🖼️

Image by a Plane Mirror

Image formed by a plane mirror is always virtual and erect. The size of the image is equal to that of the object. The image is as far behind the mirror as the object is in front of it. Further, the image is laterally inverted.

💡 Ray of light: Light seems to travel in straight lines. The fact that a small source of light casts a sharp shadow of an opaque object points to this straight-line path of light, usually indicated as a ray of light.

🥄

Concave Mirror

A spherical mirror whose reflecting surface is curved inwards, that is, faces towards the centre of the sphere. The centre of curvature lies in front of it.

The curved surface of a spoon curved inwards can be approximated to a concave mirror.

🥄

Convex Mirror

A spherical mirror whose reflecting surface is curved outwards. The centre of curvature lies behind the mirror.

The surface of the spoon bulged outwards can be approximated to a convex mirror.

02.1 · KEY TERMS

Important Terms for Spherical Mirrors

P
Pole (P)

The centre of the reflecting surface of a spherical mirror. It lies on the surface of the mirror. The pole is usually represented by the letter P.

C
Centre of Curvature (C)

The centre of the sphere of which the reflecting surface of the mirror forms a part. It is represented by the letter C. The centre of curvature is not a part of the mirror. It lies outside its reflecting surface.

R
Radius of Curvature (R)

The radius of the sphere of which the reflecting surface forms a part. Represented by letter R. The distance PC is equal to the radius of curvature.

R = 2f    (for spherical mirrors of small apertures)
PA
Principal Axis

A straight line passing through the pole and the centre of curvature of a spherical mirror. The principal axis is normal to the mirror at its pole.

F
Principal Focus (F)

For a concave mirror: rays parallel to the principal axis, after reflection, all meet/intersect at a point on the principal axis — this is the principal focus. For a convex mirror: reflected rays appear to come from a point on the principal axis — that is the principal focus.

f
Focal Length (f)

The distance between the pole and the principal focus of a spherical mirror. Represented by the letter f.

f = R/2    The principal focus lies midway between the pole and centre of curvature.
MN
Aperture

The diameter of the reflecting surface of a spherical mirror is called its aperture. Distance MN represents the aperture. We consider only spherical mirrors whose aperture is much smaller than its radius of curvature.

02.7 · USES OF MIRRORS

Uses of Concave and Convex Mirrors

🔦
Torches & Headlights
Concave mirrors are commonly used in torches, search-lights and vehicles headlights to get powerful parallel beams of light.
Mirror type: Concave mirror
🪒
Shaving Mirrors
Concave mirrors are often used as shaving mirrors to see a larger image of the face. Dentists also use concave mirrors to see large images of the teeth of patients.
Mirror type: Concave mirror
☀️
Solar Furnaces
Large concave mirrors are used to concentrate sunlight to produce heat in solar furnaces.
Mirror type: Concave mirror
🚗
Rear-view (Wing) Mirrors
Convex mirrors are commonly used as rear-view (wing) mirrors in vehicles. They give an erect, though diminished, image and have a wider field of view as they are curved outwards.
Mirror type: Convex mirror
03 · SIGN CONVENTION

New Cartesian Sign Convention

While dealing with the reflection of light by spherical mirrors, we follow a set of sign conventions called the New Cartesian Sign Convention. In this convention, the pole (P) of the mirror is taken as the origin. The principal axis of the mirror is taken as the x-axis (X′X) of the coordinate system.

i
The object is always placed to the left of the mirror. The light from the object falls on the mirror from the left-hand side.
ii
All distances parallel to the principal axis are measured from the pole of the mirror.
iii
Distances measured to the right of the origin (along +x-axis) are positive; those to the left (along −x-axis) are negative.
iv
Distances measured perpendicular to and above the principal axis (along +y-axis) are positive.
v
Distances measured perpendicular to and below the principal axis (along −y-axis) are negative.

04 · MIRROR FORMULA & MAGNIFICATION

Mirror Formula & Magnification

🔢 Mirror Formula

In a spherical mirror, the distance of the object from its pole is called the object distance (u). The distance of the image from the pole of the mirror is called the image distance (v). The distance of the principal focus from the pole is called the focal length (f).

MIRROR FORMULA (Eq. 9.1)
1/v + 1/u = 1/f
Valid for all spherical mirrors for all positions of the object.

📌 Always use the New Cartesian Sign Convention while substituting numerical values for u, v, f and R in the mirror formula.

🔎 Magnification

Magnification produced by a spherical mirror gives the relative extent to which the image of an object is magnified with respect to the object size. It is expressed as the ratio of the height of the image to the height of the object. Represented by the letter m.

MAGNIFICATION (Eq. 9.2 & 9.3)
m = h′/h = −v/u
h = height of object, h′ = height of image
+
Positive magnification

A positive sign in the value of magnification indicates that the image is virtual. Height of image is taken as positive for virtual images.

−
Negative magnification

A negative sign in the value of magnification indicates that the image is real. Height of image is taken as negative for real images.

04.1 · SOLVED EXAMPLES (NCERT)

Solved Examples — Mirror Formula

EXAMPLE 9.1 — CONVEX MIRROR (NCERT)
A convex mirror used for rear-view on an automobile has a radius of curvature of 3.00 m. If a bus is located at 5.00 m from this mirror, find the position, nature and size of the image.
Radius of curvature, R = +3.00 m
Object-distance, u = −5.00 m
Image-distance, v = ?   Height of the image, h′ = ?
Step 1: Find focal length:
f = R/2 = +3.00/2 = +1.50 m   (principal focus of convex mirror is behind the mirror)
Step 2: Apply mirror formula 1/v + 1/u = 1/f:
1/v = 1/f − 1/u = 1/1.50 − 1/(−5.00) = 1/1.50 + 1/5.00
1/v = (5.00 + 1.50)/7.50 = 6.50/7.50
v = +7.50/6.50 = +1.15 m
Step 3: Find magnification:
m = −v/u = −(+1.15)/(−5.00) = +0.23
✅ The image is 1.15 m at the back of the mirror.
The image is virtual, erect and smaller in size by a factor of 0.23.
EXAMPLE 9.2 — CONCAVE MIRROR (NCERT)
An object, 4.0 cm in size, is placed at 25.0 cm in front of a concave mirror of focal length 15.0 cm. At what distance from the mirror should a screen be placed in order to obtain a sharp image? Find the nature and the size of the image.
Object-size, h = +4.0 cm
Object-distance, u = −25.0 cm
Focal length, f = −15.0 cm
Image-distance, v = ?   Image-size, h′ = ?
Step 1: Apply mirror formula 1/v + 1/u = 1/f:
1/v = 1/f − 1/u = 1/(−15.0) − 1/(−25.0) = −1/15.0 + 1/25.0
1/v = (−5.0 + 3.0)/75.0 = −2.0/75.0
v = −37.5 cm
Step 2: Find size of image (magnification):
h′ = −(v/u) × h = −[(−37.5)/(−25.0)] × 4.0 = −6.0 cm
✅ The screen should be placed at 37.5 cm in front of the mirror.
Height of image h′ = −6.0 cm. The image is real, inverted and enlarged.
05 · REFRACTION OF LIGHT

Refraction of Light & Laws of Refraction

🌊

Refraction of Light

When travelling obliquely from one medium to another, the direction of propagation of light in the second medium changes. This phenomenon is known as refraction of light.

Refraction is due to change in the speed of light as it enters from one transparent medium to another.

➡️

Rarer to Denser Medium

When a ray of light travels from a rarer medium to a denser medium, it slows down and bends towards the normal. Example: air to glass — the light ray bends towards the normal.

↗️

Denser to Rarer Medium

When a ray of light travels from a denser medium to a rarer medium, it speeds up and bends away from the normal. Example: glass to air — the light ray bends away from the normal.

05.1 · LAWS OF REFRACTION

Laws of Refraction & Snell's Law

📜 The Two Laws of Refraction

i
First Law of Refraction

The incident ray, the refracted ray and the normal to the interface of two transparent media at the point of incidence, all lie in the same plane.

ii
Second Law — Snell's Law of Refraction

The ratio of sine of angle of incidence to the sine of angle of refraction is a constant, for the light of a given colour and for the given pair of media. (This is true for angle 0 < i < 90°)

sin i / sin r = constant
SNELL'S LAW (Eq. 9.4)
sin i / sin r = n₂₁
This constant value is called the refractive index of the second medium with respect to the first.

🔳 Refraction through a Rectangular Glass Slab

In a rectangular glass slab, the light ray at point O enters from a rarer medium to a denser medium (air to glass) and bends towards the normal. At O′, the light enters from glass to air (denser to rarer) and bends away from the normal.

1
Incident Ray (EO)

Ray obliquely incident on surface AB. Bends towards normal on entering glass (rarer to denser).

2
Refracted Ray (OO′)

Ray travelling inside the glass slab.

3
Emergent Ray (O′H)

The emergent ray is parallel to the direction of the incident ray. The extent of bending at opposite parallel faces AB (air-glass) and CD (glass-air) is equal and opposite. However, the light ray is shifted sideward slightly.

06 · REFRACTIVE INDEX

The Refractive Index

📖 What is Refractive Index?

The extent of the change in direction that takes place in a given pair of media may be expressed in terms of the refractive index. It can be linked to the relative speed of propagation of light in different media.

REFRACTIVE INDEX (Eq. 9.5)
n₂₁ = v₁ / v₂
Speed of light in medium 1 / Speed of light in medium 2
ABSOLUTE REFRACTIVE INDEX (Eq. 9.7)
nₘ = c / v
Speed of light in air / Speed of light in medium. Called the absolute refractive index.
💡
Light travels fastest in vacuum

Speed of light in vacuum = 3×10⁸ m s⁻¹. In air, it is only marginally less. It reduces considerably in glass or water.

🔍
Optical Density ≠ Mass Density

An optically denser medium may not possess greater mass density. Example: kerosene (n=1.44) is optically denser than water (n=1.33), although its mass density is less than water.

07 · LENSES

Refraction by Spherical Lenses

🔍

Convex Lens (Double Convex)

A transparent material bound by two surfaces, of which one or both surfaces are spherical. A lens with two spherical surfaces bulging outwards is called a double convex lens, simply called a convex lens. It is thicker at the middle as compared to the edges.

Convex lens converges light rays. Hence also called converging lens. Focal length is positive.

🔬

Concave Lens (Double Concave)

A double concave lens is bounded by two spherical surfaces, curved inwards. It is thicker at the edges than at the middle. Such lenses diverge light rays.

Also called diverging lens. A double concave lens is simply called a concave lens. Focal length is negative.

07.1 · KEY TERMS FOR LENSES

Important Terms for Lenses

C
Centres of Curvature (C₁ and C₂)

Each surface of a lens forms a part of a sphere. The centres of these spheres are called centres of curvature of the lens. Represented by letters C₁ and C₂.

PA
Principal Axis

An imaginary straight line passing through the two centres of curvature of a lens is called its principal axis.

O
Optical Centre (O)

The central point of a lens is its optical centre. Usually represented by the letter O. A ray of light through the optical centre of a lens passes without suffering any deviation.

F
Principal Focus (F₁ and F₂)

For a convex lens: rays parallel to the principal axis converge to a point on the principal axis — the principal focus. For a concave lens: rays appear to diverge from a point on the principal axis. A lens has two principal foci, represented by F₁ and F₂.

f
Focal Length (f)

The distance of the principal focus from the optical centre of a lens is called its focal length. Represented by letter f.

ap
Aperture

The effective diameter of the circular outline of a spherical lens is called its aperture. We consider thin lenses with small apertures whose aperture is much less than its radius of curvature.

07.4 · SOLVED EXAMPLES (NCERT)

Solved Examples — Lens Formula

EXAMPLE 9.3 — CONCAVE LENS (NCERT)
A concave lens has focal length of 15 cm. At what distance should the object from the lens be placed so that it forms an image at 10 cm from the lens? Also, find the magnification produced by the lens.
Image-distance, v = −10 cm  (image on same side as object for concave lens)
Focal length, f = −15 cm
Object-distance, u = ?
A concave lens always forms a virtual, erect image on the same side of the object.
Step 1: Apply lens formula 1/v − 1/u = 1/f, rearrange for u:
1/u = 1/v − 1/f = 1/(−10) − 1/(−15) = −1/10 + 1/15
1/u = (−3 + 2)/30 = −1/30   →  u = −30 cm
Step 2: Find magnification:
m = v/u = (−10)/(−30) = +1/3 ≈ +0.33
✅ Object-distance is 30 cm.
The positive sign shows that the image is erect and virtual. The image is one-third of the size of the object.
EXAMPLE 9.4 — CONVEX LENS (NCERT)
A 2.0 cm tall object is placed perpendicular to the principal axis of a convex lens of focal length 10 cm. The distance of the object from the lens is 15 cm. Find the nature, position and size of the image. Also find its magnification.
Height of object, h = +2.0 cm
Focal length, f = +10 cm
Object-distance, u = −15 cm
Image-distance, v = ?    Height of image, h′ = ?
Step 1: Apply lens formula 1/v − 1/u = 1/f:
1/v = 1/u + 1/f = 1/(−15) + 1/10 = −1/15 + 1/10 = (−2 + 3)/30 = 1/30
v = +30 cm
The positive sign of v shows that the image is formed at a distance of 30 cm on the other side of the optical centre. The image is real and inverted.
Step 2: Find height of image:
h′ = h × (v/u) = 2.0 × (+30/−15) = −4.0 cm
Step 3: Find magnification:
m = v/u = +30/(−15) = −2
✅ The negative signs of m and h′ show that the image is inverted and real. It is formed below the principal axis.
A real, inverted image, 4 cm tall, is formed at a distance of 30 cm on the other side of the lens. The image is two times enlarged.

07.4 · LENS FORMULA & MAGNIFICATION

Lens Formula & Magnification

🔢 Lens Formula

The lens formula gives the relationship between object-distance (u), image-distance (v) and the focal length (f). For lenses, all measurements are taken from the optical centre of the lens.

LENS FORMULA (Eq. 9.8)
1/v − 1/u = 1/f
General and valid for any spherical lens in all situations.

📌 Sign convention for lenses: Focal length of a convex lens is positive and that of a concave lens is negative.

🔎 Magnification by a Lens

The magnification produced by a lens is defined as the ratio of the height of the image and the height of the object.

LENS MAGNIFICATION (Eq. 9.9 & 9.10)
m = h′/h = v/u
h = height of object, h′ = height of image given by a lens
+
Positive m

Image is erect and virtual.

−
Negative m

Image is inverted and real.

08 · POWER OF A LENS

Power of a Lens

⚡ What is Power of a Lens?

The degree of convergence or divergence of light rays achieved by a lens is expressed in terms of its power. The power of a lens is defined as the reciprocal of its focal length. It is represented by the letter P.

POWER OF A LENS (Eq. 9.11)
P = 1/f
f must be expressed in metres for power in dioptres.
+
Convex lens — Positive power

The power of a convex lens is positive.

−
Concave lens — Negative power

The power of a concave lens is negative.

📏 The Dioptre

The SI unit of power of a lens is 'dioptre'. It is denoted by the letter D. If f is expressed in metres, then power is expressed in dioptres.

📌 1 dioptre is the power of a lens whose focal length is 1 metre. 1D = 1m⁻¹.

Refraction of lightThe phenomenon in which, when travelling obliquely from one medium to another, the direction of propagation of light in the second medium changes. Refraction is due to change in the speed of light.
Laws of Refraction(i) The incident ray, the refracted ray and the normal to the interface at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane. (ii) sin i / sin r = constant for light of a given colour and for the given pair of media (Snell's law).
Snell's lawThe ratio of sine of angle of incidence to the sine of angle of refraction is a constant, for the light of a given colour and for the given pair of media.
Refractive indexThe constant in Snell's law — the ratio of the speed of light in one medium to the speed in another. The absolute refractive index nₘ = c/v (speed of light in air / speed in medium).
Absolute refractive indexThe refractive index of a medium with respect to vacuum (or air). nₘ = c/v. Simply called the refractive index of the medium.
Optical densityThe ability of a medium to refract light. A medium with a larger refractive index is optically denser. Not the same as mass density.
Convex lensA double convex lens — two spherical surfaces bulging outwards. Thicker at the middle. Converges light rays. Also called converging lens. Focal length is positive.
Concave lensA double concave lens — bounded by two spherical surfaces curved inwards. Thicker at the edges. Diverges light rays. Also called diverging lens. Focal length is negative.
Optical centre (O)The central point of a lens. A ray of light through the optical centre of a lens passes without suffering any deviation.
Lens formula1/v − 1/u = 1/f. Gives the relationship between object-distance (u), image-distance (v) and focal length (f). General and valid for any spherical lens.
Power of a lens (P)The degree of convergence or divergence of light rays achieved by a lens. Defined as the reciprocal of its focal length. P = 1/f. Power of a convex lens is positive; concave lens is negative.
Dioptre (D)The SI unit of power of a lens. 1 dioptre is the power of a lens whose focal length is 1 metre. 1D = 1m⁻¹.

  7. We wish to obtain an erect image of an object, using a concave mirror of focal length 15 cm. What should be the range of distance of the object from the mirror? What is the nature of the image? Is the image larger or smaller than the object? Draw a ray diagram to show the image formation in this case.
    The object should be placed between the pole (P) and the principal focus (F) of the concave mirror, i.e., at a distance less than 15 cm from the mirror. The image formed is virtual and erect. The image is larger (magnified) than the object. (Ray diagram: object between P and F → image forms behind the mirror, enlarged, virtual and erect.)
  8. Name the type of mirror used in the following situations: (a) Headlights of a car. (b) Side/rear-view mirror of a vehicle. (c) Solar furnace. Support your answer with reason.
    (a) Concave mirror — used in headlights of a car to get powerful parallel beams of light. The bulb is placed at the focus of the concave mirror; reflected light emerges as a parallel beam. (b) Convex mirror — used as rear-view (wing) mirror. Convex mirrors always give an erect, though diminished image, and have a wider field of view, enabling the driver to see a larger area of the traffic behind. (c) Concave mirror — large concave mirrors are used to concentrate sunlight to produce heat in solar furnaces.
  9. One-half of a convex lens is covered with a black paper. Will this lens produce a complete image of the object? Verify your answer experimentally. Explain your observations.
    Yes, the lens will still produce a complete image of the object, but the image will be less bright (dimmer). This is because the uncovered half of the lens still refracts light rays from all parts of the object and forms a complete image. However, since fewer light rays pass through the lens, the intensity of the image is reduced compared to when the full lens is used.
  10. An object 5 cm in length is held 25 cm away from a converging lens of focal length 10 cm. Draw the ray diagram and find the position, size and the nature of the image formed.
    Given: h = +5 cm, u = −25 cm, f = +10 cm. Using lens formula: 1/v − 1/u = 1/f → 1/v = 1/f + 1/u = 1/10 + 1/(−25) = 1/10 − 1/25 = 5/50 − 2/50 = 3/50 → v = +50/3 ≈ +16.7 cm. The image is formed 16.7 cm on the other side of the lens. Magnification m = v/u = (50/3)/(−25) = −2/3. Height of image h′ = m × h = −2/3 × 5 = −10/3 ≈ −3.3 cm. The image is real, inverted and diminished (smaller than the object).
  11. A concave lens of focal length 15 cm forms an image 10 cm from the lens. How far is the object placed from the lens? Draw the ray diagram.
    Given: f = −15 cm, v = −10 cm (image on same side as object for concave lens), u = ? Using lens formula: 1/v − 1/u = 1/f → 1/u = 1/v − 1/f = 1/(−10) − 1/(−15) = −1/10 + 1/15 = −3/30 + 2/30 = −1/30. So u = −30 cm. The object is placed 30 cm in front of the lens.
  12. An object is placed at a distance of 10 cm from a convex mirror of focal length 15 cm. Find the position and nature of the image.
    Given: u = −10 cm, f = +15 cm (convex mirror). Using mirror formula: 1/v + 1/u = 1/f → 1/v = 1/f − 1/u = 1/15 − 1/(−10) = 1/15 + 1/10 = 2/30 + 3/30 = 5/30 → v = +6 cm. The image is formed 6 cm behind the mirror. The image is virtual and erect (positive v for mirror means image is behind the mirror).
  13. The magnification produced by a plane mirror is +1. What does this mean?
    The magnification of +1 means: (i) The size of the image is equal to the size of the object (|m| = 1 means same size). (ii) The positive sign indicates that the image is virtual and erect. So the plane mirror forms a virtual, erect image of the same size as the object.
  14. Find the focal length of a lens of power −2.0 D. What type of lens is this?
    Power P = −2.0 D. Using P = 1/f → f = 1/P = 1/(−2.0) = −0.5 m = −50 cm. The focal length is −50 cm. Since the focal length is negative, this is a concave lens (diverging lens).
  15. A doctor has prescribed a corrective lens of power +1.5 D. Find the focal length of the lens. Is the prescribed lens diverging or converging?
    Power P = +1.5 D. Using P = 1/f → f = 1/P = 1/(+1.5) = +0.667 m ≈ +66.7 cm. The focal length is positive (+66.7 cm). Since the power is positive and the focal length is positive, this is a convex lens. A convex lens is a converging lens. So the prescribed lens is converging.
