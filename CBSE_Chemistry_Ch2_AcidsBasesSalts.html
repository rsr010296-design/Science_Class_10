01 · CHEMICAL PROPERTIES

Properties of Acids and Bases

🍋

Acids

Sour in taste. Turn blue litmus red. Produce H⁺(aq) ions in water. Conduct electricity in aqueous solution.

Examples: HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃, CH₃COOH (acetic acid)

🧼

Bases

Bitter in taste, soapy to touch. Turn red litmus blue. Produce OH⁻(aq) ions in water. Bases soluble in water are called alkalis.

Examples: NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)₂, Mg(OH)₂

🔬

Indicators

Substances that change colour in acidic or basic media. Litmus (natural), phenolphthalein and methyl orange (synthetic) are common indicators.

Olfactory indicators: Onion, clove oil, vanilla

01.1 · REACTIONS OF ACIDS

Acids React with Metals, Carbonates & Bases

1
Acid + Metal → Salt + Hydrogen gas ↑

The metal displaces hydrogen from the acid. Hydrogen gas burns with a pop sound.

Zn(s) + H₂SO₄(aq) → ZnSO₄(aq) + H₂(g)↑
2Na(s) + 2HCl(aq) → 2NaCl(aq) + H₂(g)↑
2
Acid + Metal Carbonate → Salt + CO₂ + Water

CO₂ gas turns lime water milky. This is used to test for CO₂.

Na₂CO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → 2NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)↑
NaHCO₃(s) + HCl(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)↑
3
Acid + Base → Salt + Water (Neutralisation)

The effect of an acid is nullified by a base and vice versa. H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O

NaOH(aq) + HCl(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)
4
Metal Oxide + Acid → Salt + Water

Metallic oxides are basic oxides. Copper oxide + HCl gives blue-green copper chloride solution.

CuO(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CuCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l)
5
Base + Metal → Salt + Hydrogen gas ↑

Not all metals react with bases. Zinc reacts with NaOH to form sodium zincate.

2NaOH(aq) + Zn(s) → Na₂ZnO₂(aq) + H₂(g)↑
6
Non-metallic Oxide + Base → Salt + Water

Non-metallic oxides are acidic in nature. CO₂ reacts with Ca(OH)₂ (lime water).

Ca(OH)₂(aq) + CO₂(g) → CaCO₃(s)↓ + H₂O(l)

02 · IONS IN SOLUTION

What do Acids and Bases have in Common?

🔴 Acids produce H⁺(aq) ions

All acids dissociate in water to produce hydronium ions (H₃O⁺). This is what makes them acidic.

DISSOCIATION IN WATER
HCl + H₂O → H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻
H₂SO₄ + 2H₂O → 2H₃O⁺ + SO₄²⁻
H⁺ ions cannot exist alone — they always exist as H₃O⁺ (hydronium ion)

⚠ Key NCERT point: Dry HCl gas does NOT show acidic behaviour because H⁺ ions are produced only in the presence of water.

⚠ Why dilute acid in water? Always add acid to water (not water to acid). Dissolving acid is highly exothermic — adding water to acid can cause splashing and burns.

🔵 Bases produce OH⁻(aq) ions

All bases dissociate in water to produce hydroxide ions. Bases soluble in water are called alkalis.

DISSOCIATION IN WATER
NaOH → Na⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)
KOH → K⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)
Mg(OH)₂ → Mg²⁺(aq) + 2OH⁻(aq)

💡 Alkali = a base that dissolves in water. They are soapy to touch, bitter and corrosive. All alkalis are bases, but not all bases are alkalis.

🧪 Dilution: Mixing an acid or base with water decreases the concentration of H₃O⁺/OH⁻ ions per unit volume. This process is called dilution.

03 · pH SCALE

The pH Scale — Measuring Strength

📉

pH < 7

Acidic solution. Higher the H⁺ concentration, lower the pH. pH = 1 is very strongly acidic.

⚖️

pH = 7

Neutral solution. Pure water has pH = 7. Equal concentrations of H⁺ and OH⁻.

📈

pH > 7

Basic (alkaline) solution. Higher the OH⁻ concentration, higher the pH. pH = 14 is very strongly basic.

💡 What is pH? pH stands for 'potenz' (German for power). The pH scale (0–14) measures hydrogen ion concentration. A universal indicator shows different colours at different pH values. Higher H₃O⁺ concentration → lower pH value.

03.1 · IMPORTANCE

pH in Everyday Life

🦷
Tooth Decay

Tooth decay starts when the pH of the mouth falls below 5.5. Bacteria present in the mouth produce acids by degradation of sugar and food particles.

Tooth enamel is made of calcium hydroxyapatite — the hardest substance in the body. It does not dissolve in water but is corroded at pH below 5.5.

Remedy: Use basic toothpastes to neutralise excess acid.
🌧️
Acid Rain

When the pH of rain water is less than 5.6, it is called acid rain. It flows into rivers, lowering their pH and making survival of aquatic life difficult.

Our body works within the pH range of 7.0 to 7.8. Living organisms can survive only in a narrow range of pH change.

Normal rain: pH ~5.6 | Acid rain: pH < 5.6
🍽️
Indigestion

Our stomach produces hydrochloric acid which helps in digestion. During indigestion, the stomach produces too much acid causing pain and irritation.

People use antacids (bases) to neutralise the excess acid. Magnesium hydroxide (Milk of magnesia) is a mild base commonly used.

Mg(OH)₂ + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + 2H₂O
🐝
Bee Sting

A bee sting injects an acid (methanoic acid/formic acid) which causes pain and irritation. Applying a mild base like baking soda gives relief.

Stinging hair of nettle leaves also inject methanoic acid causing burning pain. The traditional remedy is rubbing with the dock plant leaf (basic in nature).

Bee sting: Methanoic acid (HCOOH)
🌱
Soil pH

Plants require a specific pH range for healthy growth. If the soil is too acidic, farmers treat it with quick lime (CaO), slaked lime Ca(OH)₂ or chalk (CaCO₃).

The universal indicator paper is used to measure soil pH.

Ideal soil pH for most crops: 6.0 – 7.0
04 · SALTS

More About Salts

pH of Salts

The pH of a salt depends on the strength of the acid and base used to make it.

SALT OF STRONG ACID + STRONG BASE
NaCl (from HCl + NaOH) → pH = 7 (Neutral)
SALT OF STRONG ACID + WEAK BASE
NH₄Cl (from HCl + NH₄OH) → pH < 7 (Acidic)
SALT OF WEAK ACID + STRONG BASE
Na₂CO₃ (from H₂CO₃ + NaOH) → pH > 7 (Basic)

Family of Salts

Salts having the same positive or negative radicals are said to belong to a family.

🧂 Sodium family: NaCl, Na₂SO₄, Na₂CO₃, NaHCO₃ (all have Na⁺)

🧂 Chloride family: NaCl, KCl, MgCl₂, CaCl₂ (all have Cl⁻)

🧂 Sulphate family: Na₂SO₄, CuSO₄, ZnSO₄, MgSO₄ (all have SO₄²⁻)

💡 Water of Crystallisation: The fixed number of water molecules present in one formula unit of a salt. E.g., CuSO₄·5H₂O (blue crystals → white on heating)

NaHCO₃

Baking Soda

Sodium Hydrogencarbonate. Mild, non-corrosive basic salt. Used in cooking, antacids, fire extinguishers.

Na₂CO₃·10H₂O

Washing Soda

Sodium Carbonate decahydrate. Basic salt. Used in glass, soap, paper industries. Removes hardness of water.

Ca(OH)₂

Slaked Lime

Calcium Hydroxide. Used for whitewashing walls, making cement. Reacts slowly with CO₂ to form CaCO₃.

Ca(ClO)₂

Bleaching Powder

Calcium hypochlorite. Used for bleaching textiles, disinfecting water, oxidising agent.

CuSO₄·5H₂O

Blue Vitriol

Hydrated copper sulphate. Blue crystals. Turns white (anhydrous) on heating. Blue colour restored on adding water.

05 · CHEMICALS FROM COMMON SALT

NaCl — The Raw Material

🧂 Common salt (NaCl) is an important raw material for sodium hydroxide, baking soda, washing soda, bleaching powder and many more chemicals of daily use.

1
Chlor-Alkali Process → NaOH + Cl₂ + H₂

When electricity is passed through an aqueous solution of NaCl (brine), it decomposes. This is called the chlor-alkali process. Cl₂ at anode, H₂ at cathode, NaOH near cathode.

2NaCl(aq) + 2H₂O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + Cl₂(g)↑ + H₂(g)↑
2
Bleaching Powder → Ca(ClO)₂

Chlorine gas from chlor-alkali process reacts with dry slaked lime Ca(OH)₂ to form bleaching powder. Used for bleaching cotton/linen, disinfecting water.

2Ca(OH)₂ + 2Cl₂ → Ca(ClO)₂ + CaCl₂ + 2H₂O
3
Baking Soda → NaHCO₃

Sodium hydrogencarbonate. Produced from NaCl. On heating → Na₂CO₃ + H₂O + CO₂. Used in cooking (CO₂ makes bread soft and spongy), antacids, fire extinguishers.

NaCl + H₂O + CO₂ + NH₃ → NH₄Cl + NaHCO₃
2NaHCO₃ →(heat) Na₂CO₃ + H₂O + CO₂
4
Washing Soda → Na₂CO₃·10H₂O

Recrystallisation of sodium carbonate gives washing soda. Basic salt. Used in glass, soap, paper industries; to remove permanent hardness of water.

Na₂CO₃ + 10H₂O → Na₂CO₃·10H₂O (Washing soda)
5
Plaster of Paris → CaSO₄·½H₂O

Gypsum (CaSO₄·2H₂O) heated at 373 K → Plaster of Paris. On mixing with water, it sets hard (reverts to gypsum). Used for fractured bones, toys, smooth surfaces.

CaSO₄·2H₂O →(373K) CaSO₄·½H₂O + 1½H₂O
CaSO₄·½H₂O + 1½H₂O → CaSO₄·2H₂O (Gypsum)
06 · IMPORTANT KEYWORDS

Keywords & Definitions (as per NCERT)

KEYWORDNCERT DEFINITION
IndicatorA dye or mixture of dyes used to indicate the presence of acids or bases by a change in colour (or odour for olfactory indicators).
Olfactory IndicatorSubstances whose odour changes in acidic or basic media. Examples: onion, clove oil, vanilla essence.
NeutralisationThe reaction between an acid and a base to give a salt and water. H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l).
AlkaliA base that dissolves in water. All alkalis are bases, but not all bases are alkalis.
Hydronium ionH₃O⁺ ion formed when H⁺ combines with water (H⁺ + H₂O → H₃O⁺). Hydrogen ions must always be shown as H⁺(aq) or H₃O⁺.
pH scaleA scale for measuring hydrogen ion concentration in a solution, generally from 0 (very acidic) to 14 (very alkaline). 'p' stands for 'potenz' (German for power).
Universal IndicatorA mixture of several indicators that shows different colours at different concentrations of hydrogen ions in a solution.
Strong acid/baseAcids that give rise to more H⁺ ions are strong acids. Bases giving more OH⁻ ions are strong bases.
Weak acid/baseAcids that give less H⁺ ions are weak acids. Example: acetic acid (CH₃COOH). Bases giving fewer OH⁻ ions are weak bases.
DilutionMixing an acid or base with water decreases the concentration of H₃O⁺/OH⁻ ions per unit volume. The acid or base is said to be diluted.
Water of CrystallisationThe fixed number of water molecules present in one formula unit of a salt. Example: CuSO₄·5H₂O has 5 water molecules.
Chlor-alkali processThe process of passing electricity through brine (aqueous NaCl) to produce NaOH, Cl₂ and H₂. Named because of products — 'chlor' for chlorine, 'alkali' for NaOH.
Bleaching powderCa(ClO)₂ — produced by the action of chlorine on dry slaked lime. Used for bleaching cotton/linen, disinfecting water.
Baking sodaSodium hydrogencarbonate (NaHCO₃). A mild non-corrosive basic salt. On heating produces CO₂ which makes bread/cake soft and spongy.
Washing sodaSodium carbonate decahydrate (Na₂CO₃·10H₂O). A basic salt used in glass, soap, paper industries and for removing permanent hardness of water.
Plaster of ParisCaSO₄·½H₂O — calcium sulphate hemihydrate. Obtained by heating gypsum (CaSO₄·2H₂O) at 373 K. Sets hard with water to form gypsum again.
GypsumCaSO₄·2H₂O — calcium sulphate dihydrate. Has 2 water molecules of crystallisation.
AntacidA mild base used to neutralise excess acid in the stomach and provide relief during indigestion. Example: Milk of magnesia — Mg(OH)₂.
BrineAn aqueous solution of sodium chloride (NaCl). Used in the chlor-alkali process.
Acid rainRain water with pH less than 5.6. Caused by dissolved acidic gases. Harms aquatic life by lowering river pH.
Basic oxidesMetallic oxides that react with acids to give salts and water, similar to the reaction of a base with an acid.
Acidic oxidesNon-metallic oxides (e.g. CO₂) that react with bases to give salts and water. Non-metallic oxides are acidic in nature.
