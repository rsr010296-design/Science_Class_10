01 · ECOSYSTEM

What is an Ecosystem?

🌿

Ecosystem

All interacting organisms in an area together with the non-living constituents of the environment form an ecosystem. It has biotic and abiotic components.

Examples: forests, ponds, lakes, gardens, crop-fields

🌡️

Abiotic Components

Physical, non-living factors that influence ecosystems: temperature, rainfall, wind, soil and minerals.

Natural ecosystems: forests, ponds, lakes. Artificial: gardens, crop-fields, aquarium.

🌱

Biotic Components

All living organisms — plants, animals, microorganisms, humans. Classified as producers, consumers and decomposers.

Consumers: herbivores, carnivores, omnivores, parasites

🌿 Producers

Green plants and certain bacteria that produce food by photosynthesis from inorganic substances using solar energy. They are at the first trophic level.

💡 Only about 1% of sunlight energy that falls on leaves is captured and converted into food energy by green plants.

🍄 Decomposers

Microorganisms — mainly bacteria and fungi — that break down the dead remains and waste products of organisms. They break down the complex organic substances into simple inorganic substances that go into the soil and are used up once more by the plants.

⚠️ Without decomposers: Dead organic matter would pile up, nutrient recycling in soil would stop, and ecosystem balance would collapse.

1
Trophic Levels

Each step or level of a food chain is called a trophic level. Producers = 1st, herbivores = 2nd, small carnivores = 3rd, large carnivores = 4th trophic level.

2
Food Web

In nature, each organism is eaten by two or more organisms, and in turn eats several others. This complex interlinking of food chains forms a food web.

3
Autotrophs vs Heterotrophs

Autotrophs (producers) capture solar energy and convert it to chemical energy. Heterotrophs (consumers) depend on autotrophs directly or indirectly.

4
Why Short Food Chains?

Loss of energy at each step is so great that very little usable energy remains after four trophic levels. Food chains generally consist of only 3 to 4 steps.

🌐 A food web consists of many interlinking food chains. It is a more realistic picture of feeding relationships in nature because most organisms eat more than one type of food.

03 · ENERGY FLOW

Energy Flow & the 10% Energy Transfer

⚡ Key Principles of Energy Flow

1
Unidirectional Flow

Energy flows only in one direction — from producers to consumers. It does not reverse back to the previous level.

2
Energy Decreases at Each Level

The energy available at each trophic level gets diminished progressively. Energy is lost as heat to the environment, some goes into digestion and in doing work, and only the rest goes towards growth.

3
10% Energy Transfer

Only 10% of the food eaten is turned into body mass and made available for the next level. 90% is lost as heat, digestion, and work.

1000 J → 100 J → 10 J → 1 J (across 4 levels)
4
Plants Capture 1% of Sunlight

Green plants in a terrestrial ecosystem capture about 1% of the sunlight energy that falls on their leaves.

04 · BIOLOGICAL MAGNIFICATION

Biological Magnification

☠️ What is Biological Magnification?

Pesticides and harmful chemicals used to protect crops are washed into soil and water. They enter the food chain and get progressively concentrated at each trophic level. This is called biological magnification.

1
Entry Point

Pesticides are washed into soil/water → absorbed by plants and aquatic organisms → enter the food chain.

2
Non-Degradable Accumulation

Since these chemicals are not degradable, they accumulate progressively at each trophic level and are not excreted.

3
Humans at Greatest Risk

Humans occupy the top of food chains, so the maximum concentration of toxic chemicals accumulates in our bodies.

⚠️ Pesticide residues are found in wheat, rice, vegetables, fruits and even meat. They cannot always be removed by washing.

05 · OZONE LAYER

Ozone Layer & Its Depletion

🌍 What is the Ozone Layer?

Ozone (O₃) is a molecule formed by three atoms of oxygen. Ozone is a deadly poison. However, at the higher levels of the atmosphere, ozone performs an essential function. It shields the surface of the earth from ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the Sun. This radiation is highly damaging to organisms — it is known to cause skin cancer in human beings.

HOW OZONE FORMS IN THE STRATOSPHERE

O₂ →UV O + O

O + O₂ → O₃ (Ozone)

High-energy UV radiation splits O₂ molecules into free oxygen atoms which then combine with O₂ to form ozone.

⚠️ How is it Getting Depleted?

1
Sharp Drop in the 1980s

Ozone levels in the atmosphere began dropping sharply in the 1980s — scientists noticed a growing "hole" over Antarctica.

2
CFCs are the Culprit

Linked to synthetic chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) used as refrigerants and in fire extinguishers — they destroy ozone molecules.

Refrigerants Fire Extinguishers
3
UNEP Agreement — 1987

The United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) succeeded in forging an agreement to freeze CFC production at 1986 levels.

4
CFC-Free Refrigerators Mandatory

It is now mandatory for all manufacturing companies to make CFC-free refrigerators throughout the world.

☀️ UV Radiation Danger: UV radiation is highly damaging to organisms. It is known to cause skin cancer in human beings.

06 · WASTE MANAGEMENT

Biodegradable vs Non-Biodegradable Waste

♻️

Biodegradable

Substances broken down by biological processes — by bacteria, fungi or other microorganisms. They decompose naturally and return nutrients to the soil.

Examples: vegetable peels, fruit waste, paper, cloth, wood, food scraps, tea leaves

⚠️

Non-Biodegradable

Substances that are not broken down by biological processes — not acted upon by bacteria or other saprophytes. They persist in the environment for a long time or may harm the various members of the ecosystem.

Examples: human-made materials like plastics, metals, synthetic fibres, chemicals like DDT

🏭

Waste Problem

Improved lifestyle → greater waste generation. More disposable items. Changes in packaging → more non-biodegradable waste. Serious environmental impact.

Sewage, industrial waste, e-waste, plastic waste are major concerns

06.1 · REAL-WORLD ENVIRONMENT ISSUES

Our Impact on the Environment

🌾
Pesticides in Food

Pesticides used to protect crops enter the food chain through soil and water. Due to biological magnification, they reach dangerous levels in food grains, vegetables, and meat.

Our food grains such as wheat and rice, vegetables and fruits, and even meat, contain varying amounts of pesticide residues that cannot always be removed by washing
🌊
Water Body Pollution

Untreated sewage and industrial waste discharged into water bodies pollutes rivers and lakes. Aquatic food chains absorb these pollutants, endangering all dependent life.

Solution: Sewage treatment plants before water body discharge
♻️
Disposable Culture

From plastic cups to electronic gadgets, our disposable lifestyle generates enormous non-biodegradable waste. E-waste contains hazardous materials like mercury and lead.

Kulhads → Plastic cups → Paper cups: a historical shift in waste impact
🔬
E-Waste Hazard

Electronic items we use daily, such as phones and computers, contain hazardous materials. Improper disposal of e-waste can pollute soil and water, causing serious harm to the environment.

NCERT notes that electronic items contain hazardous materials that must be disposed of carefully to protect the environment
🌍
Ozone Hole — Antarctica

The ozone hole discovered over Antarctica is evidence of CFC damage. International agreements have slowed CFC emissions and the ozone layer shows signs of slow recovery.

UNEP 1987 agreement: global CFC production freeze — ozone layer showing signs of slow recovery
🏫
What You Can Do

Use cloth bags, switch off unused lights and fans, walk or cycle instead of taking vehicles, segregate biodegradable and non-biodegradable waste at home.

Segregate biodegradable and non-biodegradable waste at home and dispose of them responsibly

  1. Which of the following groups contain only biodegradable items?
    (a) Grass, flowers and leather
    (b) Grass, wood and plastic
    (c) Fruit-peels, cake and lime-juice
    (d) Cake, wood and grass
  2. Which of the following constitute a food-chain?
    (a) Grass, wheat and mango
    (b) Grass, goat and human
    (c) Goat, cow and elephant
    (d) Grass, fish and goat
  3. Which of the following are environment-friendly practices?
    (a) Carrying cloth-bags to put purchases in while shopping
    (b) Switching off unnecessary lights and fans
    (c) Walking to school instead of getting your mother to drop you on her scooter
    (d) All of the above
  4. What will happen if we kill all the organisms in one trophic level?
    The entire food chain or food web will be disturbed. The organisms at the trophic level above will lose their source of food and their numbers will decrease. The organisms at the trophic level below will no longer be eaten and will increase in number (overpopulation). This imbalance will affect all the other trophic levels in the ecosystem and may eventually lead to its collapse.
  5. Will the impact of removing all the organisms in a trophic level be different for different trophic levels? Can the organisms of any trophic level be removed without causing any damage to the ecosystem?
    Yes, the impact will be different for different trophic levels. Removing producers (first trophic level) will be the most damaging as all other trophic levels depend directly or indirectly on them — the entire ecosystem will collapse. Removing larger carnivores (tertiary consumers) has a less immediate but still significant effect. No organism from any trophic level can be removed without causing some damage to the ecosystem, because all organisms are interconnected through food chains and food webs.
  6. What is biological magnification? Will the levels of this magnification be different at different levels of the ecosystem?
    The progressive accumulation of non-degradable harmful chemicals (such as pesticides like DDT) at each trophic level in a food chain is called biological magnification. Since these chemicals are not broken down and cannot be excreted, they accumulate in the body of organisms. Yes, the levels of magnification differ at different trophic levels — the concentration increases as we move up the food chain. Producers have the lowest concentration, and organisms at the top trophic level (such as human beings) have the highest concentration of these chemicals in their bodies.
  7. What are the problems caused by the non-biodegradable wastes that we generate?
    (i) Non-biodegradable wastes persist in the environment for a very long time and cause pollution of soil and water bodies. (ii) They may enter food chains and undergo biological magnification, harming organisms at higher trophic levels. (iii) They may release toxic substances that harm plants, animals and human beings. (iv) They can block drains, choke soil pores and reduce soil fertility. (v) They harm marine and terrestrial wildlife that may accidentally ingest them.
  8. If all the waste we generate is biodegradable, will this have no impact on the environment?
    No. Even if all waste is biodegradable, it will still impact the environment if generated in very large quantities. Large amounts of biodegradable waste will put pressure on decomposers — they may not be able to break it all down. This can lead to: (i) foul odours and unhygienic conditions; (ii) depletion of oxygen in soil and water bodies, harming organisms; (iii) breeding of disease-causing organisms; (iv) disturbance of the natural balance of the ecosystem. So, the rate of waste generation must remain within the capacity of decomposers to break it down.
  9. Why is damage to the ozone layer a cause for concern? What steps are being taken to limit this damage?
    The ozone layer in the upper atmosphere shields the surface of the earth from ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the Sun. UV radiation is highly damaging to organisms — it is known to cause skin cancer in human beings and can harm other organisms too. Damage to the ozone layer allows more UV radiation to reach the Earth's surface, increasing health and ecological risks. Steps being taken: (i) In 1987, the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) succeeded in forging an agreement to freeze CFC production at 1986 levels. (ii) It is now mandatory for all the manufacturing companies to make CFC-free refrigerators throughout the world.
